About

A simple lens on a complicated world.

The World is a small publication about global news, data, and the trends shaping how we live, work, and govern ourselves.

What we cover

We write about the economy, politics and policy, and technology, and we publish the data series that anchor those conversations — inflation, employment, interest rates, and government spending among them.

Our goal is to be useful: clear writing, honest charts, and primary sources whenever possible. Real articles and datasets live in the News section; the rest of this page is the case for why this kind of publication matters now.

AI and the future of democracy

The question is not whether AI is good or bad. It is who directs it, and toward what ends. Technology is never neutral — its gains flow to whoever has the power to capture them, and right now that power sits with a small number of firms whose business depends on attention, advertising, and data extraction.

The threat to democracy travels through familiar channels: surveillance that chills dissent, synthetic media that fragments shared reality, opaque automation inside the state, and platforms that make editorial decisions for hundreds of millions without accountability. None of it is inevitable. Every previous technological revolution first benefited a narrow elite and only later extended its gains, and only then because ordinary people organised enough countervailing power to redirect the technology.

Supporting that redirection, in a small way, is what this site is for. Clear writing about how power flows is a precondition for the kind of organised civic response that has worked before.

Policies for the current economic era

We are not stuck because the tools are missing. We are stuck because policy has, for several decades, leaned in the direction of capital over labour, scale over competition, and automation over augmentation. The tools to lean the other way still exist.

The shortlist: equalise the tax treatment of labour and capital so the system stops subsidising automation; enforce antitrust to break up concentration in tech and adjacent sectors; modernise labour law toward portable benefits and sectoral bargaining; impose disclosure, audit, and liability rules on high-stakes AI; and fund public research that aims AI at augmenting workers rather than replacing them. None of these are radical — they are the same combination that produced shared prosperity in the middle of the last century.

What is missing is not the policy menu but the political will to use it. Citizens who can read the choices on offer are harder to convince that no choices exist, which is, again, the work this site is here to do.

Our mission, and the work each major can do

Our mission is small and specific: to make you think about the unspoken information that, quietly, controls us. AI is not the only technology out there, but we are here to tell you more about the tech. We do not believe that technology automatically translates into prosperity. So we keep asking the same questions about everything we see in the world: who lost, who gained, and who controls? What are the biggest problems to solve that no one is talking about? Which voices working to make the world better are being overlooked by those with so much concentrated power?

That rests on a larger conviction. The trajectory of any powerful technology, and the political economy around it, is shaped by the people who build, study, regulate, communicate, and use it. There is no automatic process that turns invention into broadly shared prosperity — the outcome depends on choices that thousands of practitioners make every day, and on whether ordinary people have organised enough countervailing power to make those choices accountable.

Different majors carry different parts of that work. Computer scientists and engineers decide what gets built and on whose behalf — the responsibility is to build for augmentation rather than replacement. Economists, statisticians, and finance students decide what counts as evidence and how the numbers get framed — the responsibility is honest uncertainty over dressed-up forecasts. Political scientists, lawyers, and public-policy students decide what the rules are — the responsibility is to write rules that countervail concentrations of power. Journalists, communicators, and arts and design students decide what citizens actually see — the responsibility is to refuse being a delivery mechanism for the most powerful actors in any story. Educators teach the next generation to read the world they inherit. Natural scientists, medical and health students, and humanities scholars anchor what is and is not known, and insist that questions of “should” never collapse into questions of “can.” Business and management students run the institutions that employ most of the others — the responsibility is to refuse purely extractive strategies.

No major is exempt, and no major can do this alone. Shared prosperity has never been automatic; it has been the product of organised, plural effort by people who refused to accept the first answer. This site is a place where that effort can be supported in good faith.

So I want to show that there are many ways to use AI to redistribute the world's resources — and in this case, the resource is information. Not as mass surveillance, not as monitoring, not as automation built to replace people, but as a tool that helps human beings thrive and make better decisions. That is what this website is for.
