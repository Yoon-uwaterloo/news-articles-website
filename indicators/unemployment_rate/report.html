Tracking U.S. Unemployment: What Recent Readings Reveal about Labor-Market Rebalancing

The unemployment rate remains one of the clearest high-frequency gauges of labor-market slack and, by extension, of underlying economic momentum. The dataset analyzed here combines the monthly civilian unemployment rate—both seasonally adjusted and not seasonally adjusted—with several Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) longer-run and annual projection series. Together, these data help answer a practical question: how far has the labor market moved away from the exceptionally tight conditions of 2022-23, and how close is it now to policymakers' estimate of a longer-run normal unemployment rate? In the latest observation, the seasonally adjusted unemployment rate stood at 4.3 percent in March 2026, up -0.1 percentage point from the prior month and +0.1 percentage point from a year earlier.

Background

The main indicator in the file is the Bureau of Labor Statistics unemployment rate (series UNRATE), measured monthly from January 2021 through March 2026 in seasonally adjusted terms. A parallel non-seasonally-adjusted series (UNRATENSA) covers the same period. The file also includes FOMC Summary of Economic Projections series: annual central-tendency and range forecasts for 2026-28, and meeting-level longer-run estimates from March 2021 through March 2026. Those longer-run estimates are useful benchmarks because they summarize where policymakers believe unemployment should settle in the absence of further shocks.

Over the full sample, the seasonally adjusted unemployment rate averaged 4.19 percent, with a median of 4.05 percent and a standard deviation of 0.74. The sample high was 6.4 percent in January 2021, while the low was 3.4 percent in April 2023. The latest reading of 4.3 percent is therefore +0.11 percentage point relative to the full-sample mean and +0.9 percentage points above the cycle trough.

Findings

Chart 1 shows the broad arc of post-pandemic labor-market normalization. From 6.4 percent in January 2021, the unemployment rate fell rapidly to a low of 3.4 percent in April 2023. That decline of 3.0 percentage points captures the healing phase of the labor market. Since then, the direction has shifted. The unemployment rate has drifted back up to 4.3 percent, placing it about +0.1 percentage point relative to the latest FOMC longer-run central-tendency midpoint of 4.15 percent. The non-seasonally-adjusted rate was 4.3 percent in the latest month, implying a seasonal gap of +0.0 percentage point.

Chart 2 highlights two distinct phases in the sample. The first, running through 2022, was characterized by falling unemployment and a rapid recovery from pandemic-era labor-market damage. The second, beginning around early 2024, shows a gradual but persistent firming in unemployment. On a twelve-month average basis, the unemployment rate averaged 4.31 percent over the most recent year, compared with 4.21 percent before 2024. That increase of +0.10 percentage point is not recession-sized in this sample, but it is economically meaningful because it marks a clear departure from the unusually tight conditions of 2022-23.

The projection data add perspective. Chart 3 compares the actual unemployment rate with the FOMC's longer-run range and midpoint estimates. The central-tendency midpoint was remarkably stable, moving from about 3.85 percent to 4.15 percent over the sample, while the longer-run median edged up from 4.0 percent to 4.2 percent. The implication is that most of the recent rise in actual unemployment reflects cyclical normalization toward a stable longer-run benchmark rather than a large reassessment of the economy's structural unemployment rate.

Chart 4 zooms in on the last two years, when the rebalancing became clearer. The seasonally adjusted unemployment rate moved from 3.4 percent in April 2023 to 4.3 percent in March 2026, while the not-seasonally-adjusted rate moved from 3.1 percent to 4.3 percent over the same comparison points. Recent month-to-month changes remain moderate: the latest monthly change was -0.1 percentage point, and the average monthly change over the full sample was -0.034 percentage point. Even so, the cumulative drift has been enough to move the labor market from clearly tighter-than-normal conditions to roughly in line with or slightly above longer-run estimates.

Cross-series comovement is strong. Chart 5 shows that the seasonally adjusted and not-seasonally-adjusted unemployment rates have a correlation of 0.946. That tight relationship is expected, but the remaining difference matters for short-run interpretation: the average seasonal gap in the sample was +0.02 percentage point, and the latest gap was +0.0 percentage point. For analysts monitoring turning points in real time, this means the adjusted series is better suited for inference, while the nonadjusted series still conveys the same underlying labor-market signal.

Chart 6 summarizes the distribution of monthly changes. Most observations cluster near zero, consistent with unemployment being a relatively smooth macroeconomic indicator outside of recessions. Using a simple two-standard-deviation rule, the largest monthly moves in this sample include declines of -0.4 percentage point in August 2021 and increases of +0.2 percentage point in several 2024-25 months. These are notable but modest relative to recession dynamics seen in longer historical samples. In other words, the recent increase in unemployment has been gradual rather than abrupt.

Historical Comparison

The available history begins in January 2021, so the dataset does not contain earlier recessions or the 2020 pandemic spike. Even within that narrower window, however, the current reading can be situated against two useful internal benchmarks. First, relative to the 2022-23 trough period, the latest unemployment rate is +0.9 percentage points higher. Second, relative to the pre-2024 average of 4.21 percent, the latest reading is +0.09 percentage points higher. That places current conditions much softer than the exceptionally tight labor market of 2022-23, but still close to the FOMC's estimate of a longer-run normal rate.

Caveats and Limitations

Several cautions are warranted. First, the sample begins in 2021, which limits formal recession dating and makes it impossible to compare the current episode directly with earlier business-cycle downturns using only this file. Second, unemployment data are subject to revision and seasonal-adjustment updates, so real-time signals may differ from later vintages. Third, the FOMC projection series are not forecasts in the same sense as market-implied probabilities; they are conditional judgments under each participant's assessment of appropriate policy. Finally, because the dataset centers on unemployment, it provides only a partial view of real activity and does not directly incorporate payroll growth, vacancies, hours worked, or output.

Conclusion

The dataset points to a labor market that has moved out of the unusually tight territory of 2022-23 and into a more balanced phase. The latest seasonally adjusted unemployment rate of 4.3 percent is above the cycle trough, above its pre-2024 average, and essentially at the FOMC's longer-run benchmark. Importantly, the rise has occurred through small, persistent monthly increases rather than a sharp break. That pattern is more consistent with normalization than with a sudden recession signal, although continued increases would warrant closer monitoring. For policymakers and forecasters, the main message is that slack has risen enough to matter, but not yet in a way that clearly indicates severe labor-market deterioration within the sample available here.
