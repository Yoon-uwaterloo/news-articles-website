Tracking Short-Term Interest Rates and Real Yields in Real Time: What the Latest Data Reveal

Short-term interest rates and real Treasury yields are among the clearest high-frequency indicators of the stance of monetary policy and financial conditions. The dataset analyzed here spans daily observations from January 2021 through April 30, 2026 for seven indicators: the Effective Federal Funds Rate (EFFR), the Discount Window Primary Credit Rate, the 3-month Treasury bill rate, the 1-year Treasury bill rate, the Kansas City Fed’s Measure of Policy Rate Uncertainty (KCPRU), and 5-year and 10-year inflation-indexed Treasury yields. Taken together, these series answer a practical question similar to the one posed by real-time economic monitoring exercises: how restrictive are current financial conditions relative to recent history, and what do market prices imply about the persistence of that stance?

As shown in the chart below, the EFFR rose from near zero in 2021 to a peak of 5.33 percent on July 27, 2023, before declining to 3.64 percent by April 30, 2026. That leaves the policy rate 3.56 percentage points above its 2021 average of 0.08 percent, even after the later easing cycle.

Background

The data cover several layers of the interest-rate transmission mechanism. EFFR is the market overnight rate most directly associated with Federal Reserve operating policy. The primary credit rate is an administered rate that typically sits above EFFR and helps define the upper part of the short-term policy corridor. The 3-month and 1-year Treasury bill rates provide market-based readings on current and expected near-term policy settings. KCPRU captures uncertainty about where short-term policy rates will be one year ahead, while the 5-year and 10-year Treasury inflation-indexed yields summarize real borrowing costs over medium and longer horizons.

Time coverage is broad and relatively uniform. EFFR runs from January 4, 2021 to April 30, 2026 with 1,336 non-missing observations. The primary credit rate covers January 1, 2021 to April 30, 2026 with 1,389 observations. The Treasury bill and TIPS series each run from early January 2021 to April 30, 2026 with 1,332 observations, while KCPRU contains 1,341 observations from January 4, 2021 onward.

Summary statistics reinforce the large regime shift between the low-rate period of 2021 and the elevated-rate environment that followed. The EFFR averaged 3.25 percent over the full sample, with a median of 4.33 percent and standard deviation of 2.06 percentage points. The 3-month Treasury bill rate averaged 3.26 percent, while the 1-year bill rate averaged 3.19 percent. KCPRU averaged 1.15 percentage points, with a low of 0.194 on January 4, 2021 and a peak of 2.18 on March 15, 2023. Real yields also moved sharply higher: the 5-year TIPS yield averaged 0.78 percent over the sample and the 10-year TIPS yield 1.07 percent, but both began the sample deeply negative, at roughly -1.69 percent and -0.91 percent on average in 2021.

Findings

The first striking feature of the data is the clear sequence of tightening followed by easing. The EFFR bottomed at 0.05 percent on April 30, 2021. It then climbed in discrete steps beginning in March 2022, reaching 5.33 percent in late July 2023. By the end of the sample, it had fallen to 3.64 percent. The primary credit rate moved in parallel, rising from 0.25 percent in 2021 to 5.50 percent at its peak and ending at 3.75 percent.

The chart below highlights these policy regimes. The tightening phase from March 2022 to September 2024 corresponds to the steepest and most persistent increase in the sample; the easing phase begins thereafter. This visual split is useful because it clarifies that the current setting, although below peak, remains well above the pre-tightening baseline.

Period-over-period changes show that the latest moves are modest compared with the earlier tightening cycle. As of April 30, 2026, the EFFR was unchanged week over week and month over month, but down 0.69 percentage point from a year earlier. The primary credit rate was likewise unchanged over the week and month and down 0.75 percentage point year over year. The 3-month Treasury bill rate declined 0.01 percentage point over the week, 0.02 over the month, and 0.61 over the year. By contrast, the 1-year bill rate edged up 0.02 point over the week and 0.04 over the month, though it remained 0.14 point below its year-earlier level. This combination suggests that markets were pricing somewhat greater stability or modest firming over the next year than was implied by the current overnight rate alone.

Because the dataset does not contain an explicit composite index, the most informative decomposition comes from spreads between administered and market rates. The primary credit minus EFFR spread measures the corridor between the discount window and the market overnight rate; the 3-month Treasury bill minus EFFR spread measures how far market short rates are above or below the policy rate; and the 1-year minus 3-month spread captures the short end of the term structure. Over most of the sample, the primary credit minus EFFR spread is stable, reflecting the administered nature of the corridor. The Treasury bill spreads, by contrast, vary meaningfully across regimes.

As shown below, the 1-year minus 3-month term spread widened during parts of the hiking cycle as markets priced additional tightening ahead, then narrowed as policy approached its peak and eventually moved into easing. The 3-month bill rate also occasionally traded slightly above or below EFFR, reflecting money-market conditions and expectations about the policy path.

The recent period offers a more nuanced picture. Over roughly the last eighteen months, EFFR, the 3-month bill, and the 1-year bill all drifted lower from their highs, but policy uncertainty also receded. KCPRU averaged 0.98 over the latest year, below its full-sample average of 1.15 and far below its March 2023 peak of 2.18. Yet uncertainty has not fully normalized: the latest reading, 1.195, remains about 0.80 percentage point above its 2021 mean of 0.392.

The next chart makes this contrast clear. Policy rates eased from restrictive highs, but uncertainty did not fall all the way back to the subdued levels of 2021. In that sense, the level of rates and the confidence around the path of rates improved at different speeds.

Correlations across the indicators are high, but not uniform. Weekly EFFR and the primary credit rate are effectively perfectly correlated, at 1.00, which is unsurprising given the administered relationship between them. EFFR and the 3-month bill also move closely together, with a correlation of 0.995, while the 3-month and 1-year bill rates correlate at 0.981. Real yields are also highly aligned with short rates in this sample: EFFR correlates 0.963 with the 5-year TIPS yield and 0.942 with the 10-year TIPS yield. KCPRU is positively correlated with all rate series, but less tightly so, ranging from 0.572 with the 10-year real yield to 0.754 with the 1-year bill rate.

The chart below summarizes these relationships. The especially strong association between short rates and real yields suggests that the tightening cycle affected not only nominal policy settings but also real financing conditions across maturities.

Turning points and anomalies are also visible. For policy uncertainty, the most notable episode is the March 2023 spike, when KCPRU reached 2.18 on March 15, 2023. For market rates, the 3-month bill peaked at 5.36 percent on October 6, 2023, slightly later than the EFFR peak. Real yields peaked in late 2023: the 10-year TIPS yield reached 2.52 percent on October 25, 2023 and the 5-year TIPS yield 2.59 percent on October 3, 2023. Those peaks mark the point at which restrictive policy and elevated term real rates were simultaneously at their strongest.

The distribution of weekly EFFR changes underlines another feature of the sample: most weeks showed no change, while a relatively small number of weeks captured the step-like adjustments associated with policy decisions. This is typical of an administered rate series and helps explain why the tightening cycle appears as a staircase rather than a smooth rise.

Historical Comparison

Relative to the 2021 baseline, all major rates remain elevated. The latest EFFR of 3.64 percent is 3.56 percentage points above its 2021 mean. The latest 3-month bill rate, 3.59 percent, is 3.54 points above its 2021 mean of 0.05 percent, while the 1-year bill rate is 3.47 points above its 2021 mean of 0.10 percent. These gaps indicate that even after easing, the short end of the yield curve remains far tighter than in the low-rate environment that prevailed immediately after the pandemic shock.

The comparison is even more striking for real yields. The 5-year TIPS yield averaged -1.69 percent in 2021 and now stands at 1.35 percent, a rise of 3.04 percentage points. The 10-year TIPS yield averaged -0.91 percent in 2021 and now stands at 1.94 percent, up 2.85 points. This shift from deeply negative to solidly positive real yields represents a substantial tightening in real financing conditions.

At the same time, current readings are below the sample peaks. EFFR is 1.69 percentage points below its 5.33 percent high. The 3-month bill is 1.77 points below its 5.36 percent peak. KCPRU is 0.99 point below its March 2023 high. The data therefore describe a middle ground: policy is no longer at maximum restrictiveness, but neither has it returned to the highly accommodative conditions of 2021.

Caveats and Limitations

Several cautions apply. First, the sample begins in 2021, so it does not include earlier recessions or the full pre-pandemic business cycle. As a result, historical comparisons are best interpreted as comparisons with the post-pandemic low-rate period rather than with a long-run pre-2008 benchmark. Second, some series are administered or policy-driven and therefore move in discrete steps rather than continuously. Third, KCPRU is not the policy rate itself; it is a measure of uncertainty around expected future policy rates. Fourth, missing values around holidays are standard in daily financial datasets and were excluded from the calculations. Finally, because no explicit composite index is present in the files, the decomposition shown here uses economically meaningful spreads rather than formal index weights.

Conclusion

The broad message from this dataset is that financial conditions eased from their most restrictive point, but remain materially tighter than in 2021. Overnight and Treasury bill rates are lower than their 2023 peaks, yet they remain more than 3.4 percentage points above their 2021 averages. Real yields have also stayed firmly positive, indicating that inflation-adjusted borrowing costs remain elevated. Meanwhile, policy uncertainty has receded from the March 2023 stress episode but has not fully returned to its early-2021 lows.

For policymakers and forecasters, that combination matters. It suggests that the marginal impulse from monetary tightening has diminished, but that the level of restraint embedded in current real and nominal rates is still substantial. In other words, the data point less to an abrupt shift toward accommodation than to a transition from peak restrictiveness to a still-restrictive, but gradually normalizing, rate environment.
