Tracking Real Output and Demand: What the Latest BEA Data Reveal

The dataset brings together a broad set of Bureau of Economic Analysis national accounts indicators covering quarterly and annual measures of output, income, expenditure shares, and contributions to growth. In the spirit of the Federal Reserve Bank of New York’s real-time activity monitoring work, the central question here is straightforward: how strong is underlying U.S. economic activity in the latest readings, and which parts of demand are carrying growth? The headline evidence points to continued expansion, but with more uneven quarter-to-quarter momentum than the annual averages alone would suggest.

Background

The two files contain BEA-style time series running mostly from 2021 through 2025 or early 2026, depending on the indicator. The core series for this note are quarterly real GDP growth, real final sales growth, real gross domestic income (GDI), real goods output, real services output, and expenditure-share measures for consumption, investment, government, and net exports. For quarterly growth-rate series reported at seasonally adjusted annual rates, period-over-period changes are already embedded in the published values. For level series such as real GDI and real goods and services output, quarter-over-quarter and year-over-year growth rates were computed directly from the chained-dollar levels.

The time coverage is concentrated in the post-pandemic expansion. That limits the ability to compare the latest readings to earlier full business cycles, but it still allows a comparison of current conditions with the high-growth rebound of 2021, the softer patch in 2022, and the steadier expansion observed through 2023-24.

Findings

Quarterly real GDP growth averaged 2.93 percent over the sample, with a median of 2.90 percent and a standard deviation of 2.16 percentage points. The strongest quarterly reading was 7.0 percent in 2021-04-01, while the weakest was -1.0 percent in 2022-01-01. The latest observation, 2.0 percent in 2026 Q1, is below the sample mean and well below the rebound-era highs seen in 2021, but it remains positive.

The quarterly path also shows that weakness has been episodic rather than persistent. Negative real GDP growth occurred in three quarters in this sample: 2022 Q1, 2025 Q1, and a milder setback in 2024 Q1 for several related components. Using negative quarters as a recession-style stress marker, the data suggest short-lived contractions rather than a sustained downturn. Even so, the latest sequence softened noticeably from 4.4 percent in 2025 Q3 to 0.5 percent in 2025 Q4 before edging up to 2.0 percent in 2026 Q1.

If one looks beneath the headline, services have been the steadier contributor to growth, while goods have been more cyclical. Over the overlapping sample, services contributions averaged materially above goods contributions and were less volatile. In 2025 Q1, for example, services contributed -0.09 percentage point while goods contributed -0.39 percentage point, helping explain the temporary GDP contraction. By 2025 Q3, both had turned positive again, with services contributing 2.31 percentage points and goods 2.52 percentage points.

The recent-period view underscores the loss of momentum at the end of the sample. Real GDP growth moved from -0.6 percent in 2025 Q1 to 3.8 percent in 2025 Q2 and 4.4 percent in 2025 Q3, then slowed sharply to 0.5 percent in 2025 Q4 before recovering to 2.0 percent in 2026 Q1. Real final sales, often a better guide to domestic demand because it strips out inventory effects, fell 3.2 percent in 2025 Q1, rebounded 7.5 percent in 2025 Q2, and stood at 1.6 percent in 2026 Q1. That pattern suggests a meaningful inventory or trade-related swing around early 2025 rather than a clean break in final demand.

Cross-indicator comparisons point in the same direction. Real GDI growth computed from chained-dollar levels was 0.64 percent quarter over quarter in the latest reading, while real GDP rose at a 2.0 percent annualized rate and real final sales rose 1.6 percent. Over the full overlapping sample, the correlation between quarterly real GDP growth and real final sales growth was 0.73. The correlation with real GDI quarter-over-quarter growth was lower, at 0.63, consistent with the familiar GDP-GDI measurement gap. Goods and services growth also move together, but services are visibly smoother.

The distribution of quarterly GDP growth further highlights the asymmetry of this post-2021 sample. Most observations cluster in the modest-expansion range, roughly between 0 and 4 percent, with a small number of stronger upside readings in the 5 to 7 percent range and only a few negative outliers. The sample therefore looks more like a cooling expansion than a recessionary regime.

Historical comparison

Because the data begin in 2021 for most relevant series, the historical benchmark is the post-pandemic expansion itself rather than a longer pre-pandemic record. Against that benchmark, the latest GDP reading of 2.0 percent is below the 2021-24 average of 3.21 percent. The average for the 2025 readings in the sample was 2.02 percent, indicating slower and more uneven growth than in the earlier part of the expansion. The 2022 soft patch included 1 negative quarter for real GDP growth in this dataset, compared with one negative quarter in 2025 before activity rebounded.

Compositionally, consumption remained the dominant expenditure category. Its GDP share was 68.1 percent in 2025, up from 67.9 percent in 2021. Within consumption, services rose from 44.8 percent of GDP in 2021 to 47.0 percent in 2025, while goods fell from 23.2 percent to 21.2 percent. Investment’s share eased from 17.9 percent in 2021 to 17.7 percent in 2025, government demand edged down from 17.7 percent to 17.1 percent, and net exports remained negative at -3.0 percent of GDP in 2025. These shifts are consistent with a normalization away from goods-intensive pandemic demand toward services-led spending.

Composite and component decomposition

The dataset does not contain a single weekly-style composite index analogous to the Weekly Economic Index. The closest headline aggregate is real GDP growth itself, along with published contribution series. For that reason, the decomposition in this report treats real GDP growth as the aggregate outcome and uses the available contribution series to parse movements into goods and services components. This is necessarily partial: the full national accounts decomposition would also separate consumption, fixed investment, inventories, trade, and government demand in more detail.

Even this simpler decomposition is informative. Services provided the more stable support to growth through most of the sample, whereas goods were more responsible for both the shortfalls and the rebounds. That asymmetry helps explain why final demand remained comparatively firm even when headline GDP briefly turned negative.

Caveats and limitations

  • The sample is short and starts in 2021 for most series, so “historical” comparisons refer mainly to the post-pandemic period rather than to prior recessions or the pre-2020 expansion.
  • BEA national accounts data are revised, sometimes materially, so the latest readings should be interpreted as preliminary indicators rather than fixed facts.
  • Quarterly growth rates reported at annual rates can exaggerate short-run volatility; level-based year-over-year measures often give a smoother signal.
  • The negative-quarter shading in the charts is a descriptive device, not an official recession dating procedure.

Conclusion

Overall, the data depict an economy that continued to expand through the latest observation, but at a pace below the rapid early-post-pandemic rebound. The latest real GDP reading of 2.0 percent annualized, paired with 1.6 percent growth in real final sales and a positive increase in real GDI, is more consistent with continued expansion than with broad contraction. At the same time, the sharp swings around 2025 Q1 and the below-average late-sample readings point to a narrower margin of safety. The broad message is that underlying activity has remained resilient, with services still providing the main anchor, but that the economy has become more vulnerable to quarter-to-quarter shocks in goods demand, inventories, and trade. For forecasters and policymakers, that combination argues for caution: growth has not stalled, but it has become less uniformly robust.
