Tracking Producer Prices across U.S. Industries: What the Latest Data Reveal

The producer price data in this file provide a timely read on pipeline inflation across commodities, goods-producing industries, trade, transportation, warehousing, and selected service sectors. The central question is whether upstream price pressure is easing, stabilizing, or reaccelerating. The answer matters because producer prices often foreshadow changes in consumer inflation, margins, and real activity. In this dataset, the broadest available headline measure—Producer Price Index by Commodity: All Commodities—rose to 280.3 in June 2022, fell back to 249.9 by December 2023, and then climbed to 274.1 in March 2026, leaving it near but still below the mid-2022 peak.

As shown in the chart below, the headline commodity index spans January 2021 through March 2026 and captures three distinct phases: a sharp run-up through mid-2022, a disinflationary retreat through late 2023, and a renewed climb over 2024-26.

Background: What Is in the Dataset?

The file contains 14 nonempty monthly Producer Price Index (PPI) series, all reported on a not seasonally adjusted basis, covering 2021-01-01 to 2026-03-01. The panel includes a headline commodity measure (All Commodities), broad industry aggregates such as Total Manufacturing Industries and Total Mining Industries, and service-related series including Information, Transportation Industries, Transportation and Warehousing Industries, Total Trade Industries, Total Retail Trade Industries, Total Wholesale Trade Industries, Selected Health Care Industries, and Services Less Trade, Transportation, and Warehousing.

Most series are index levels rather than rates of change, with base years that differ across series. That means comparisons of levels across sectors should be made cautiously; growth rates and normalized paths are more informative. Still, the broad co-movement across the panel is economically meaningful. The common sample covers 63 monthly observations for the populated series.

Findings: The Broad Commodity Measure Has Reaccelerated

Summary statistics point to a headline series that remains elevated relative to its own recent history. The All Commodities index averages 253.3 over the full sample, with a median of 257.0 and a standard deviation of 15.0. The minimum was 204.8 in January 2021 and the maximum is the latest reading, 280.3 in March 2026. The current level is 33.84% above January 2021 and 9.21% above the average of the pre-last-12-month history. The latest twelve-month average, 262.9, also exceeds the earlier historical average of 251.0.

The intermediate retreat after the 2022 peak was substantial. From 280.3 in June 2022 to 249.9 in December 2023, the headline measure fell -10.84%. That retrenchment resembles a producer-price disinflation episode rather than a full collapse in upstream pricing. Since that trough, however, the index has risen 9.70%, and the latest month posted a 1.78% increase, equivalent to a three-month annualized pace of 20.99%.

Sector Detail: Cyclicality Is Strongest in Mining and Goods Production

The sectoral pattern is uneven. Mining remains the most volatile component in the file: its mean is 229.5, but its standard deviation is 30.7, far above Information (4.7), Health Care (5.9), or Services Less Trade, Transportation, and Warehousing (8.1). Mining surged to a sample high of 325.8 in June 2022 and then retreated sharply before rebounding again. By contrast, Information rose steadily from 105.2 at the start of the sample to 119.0 in March 2026, with only modest month-to-month variation.

Manufacturing also carries much of the headline inflation signal. The manufacturing PPI reached 265.3 in March 2026, up 6.22% from a year earlier, while Transportation Industries rose 6.58%. Service-side measures were firmer but smoother: Selected Health Care Industries advanced 2.76% over the year, and Services Less Trade, Transportation, and Warehousing increased 2.86%.

Recent Developments: The Last Two Years Show Renewed Firming

A 24-month zoom underscores how the broad commodity index firmed again after late 2023. The headline measure was 255.1 in March 2024, 258.5 in March 2025, and 274.1 in March 2026. That sequence implies a 1.34% increase in the year to March 2025 and a further 6.03% increase in the year to March 2026. In other words, the recent data do not point to continued disinflation; they point instead to renewed upstream price pressure.

The latest reading is also well above a pre-reacceleration benchmark. Relative to the January 2021 value of 204.8, the March 2026 reading is higher by 33.84%. Since this sample begins in 2021, January 2021 is the earliest benchmark available in the data and serves as a practical stand-in for the earlier, lower-inflation regime.

Cross-Indicator Comparison and Correlation

Normalized paths show that commodity and goods-producing sectors have moved together more closely than the more stable service categories. From January 2021 to March 2026, the All Commodities index increased 33.84%, compared with 32.90% for Manufacturing, 43.95% for Mining, 33.42% for Transportation Industries, and only 13.03% for Information. Delivery and Warehouse Industries rose especially strongly, up 48.09% over the sample.

Correlations reinforce that picture. In levels, the headline series is most closely associated with Total Manufacturing Industries (correlation 0.97), Transportation Industries (0.97), and Total Mining, Utilities, and Manufacturing Industries (0.96). In monthly changes, the tightest relationship is again with Manufacturing (0.91), followed by Mining (0.61) and the aggregate mining/utilities/manufacturing series (0.59). That pattern suggests the current reacceleration is still primarily a goods-side story.

Distribution of Monthly Changes and Notable Turning Points

The distribution of monthly headline changes is right-skewed, reflecting the sharp run-up in 2021-22 and a more moderate pattern afterward. The largest monthly increase in the All Commodities index occurred in May 2022, at 3.21%, while the steepest monthly decline was in July 2022, at -2.85%. More broadly, local turning points cluster around mid-2022 peaks and late-2023 troughs, consistent with a classic inflation surge followed by partial reversal and subsequent firming.

Historical Comparison

Although the sample does not extend back to earlier recessions, it is still long enough to compare the current reading with key episodes inside the file. Relative to the June 2022 peak, the March 2026 headline reading is 2.19% lower. Relative to the December 2023 trough, it is 9.70% higher. Relative to January 2021, it is 33.84% higher. Among broad sectors, Delivery and Warehouse Industries show one of the largest cumulative increases at 48.09%, while Information remains among the least inflationary with a cumulative increase of 13.03%.

The internal ranking of current year-over-year increases is also informative. Among the major broad measures, the fastest latest year-over-year gain is in All Commodities at 6.03%, followed closely by Mining at 8.14% and Manufacturing at 6.22%. At the low end, Information increased only 1.91% and Health Care 2.76%. That spread is consistent with a relative shift from broad service inflation toward commodity- and goods-linked pressure.

Caveats and Limitations

Several limitations deserve emphasis. First, all series in use here are not seasonally adjusted, so month-to-month moves can reflect seasonal patterns as well as underlying inflation pressure. Second, the sample begins in January 2021, which prevents direct comparison with pre-2020 business cycles, earlier recessions, or pre-pandemic norms using the same file alone. Third, the series have different index base years, so level comparisons across sectors are less informative than growth rates or normalized indexes. Fourth, the contribution exercise for Total Mining, Utilities, and Manufacturing Industries is only a proxy, because the underlying utilities component is not separately available in the dataset. Finally, PPI data can be revised, so the latest readings should be interpreted as provisional.

Conclusion

The main message from this dataset is that upstream price pressure eased meaningfully after the 2022 surge but has not returned to the lower readings seen at the start of the sample. Instead, the broad commodity measure has reaccelerated to 274.1 in March 2026, rising 1.78% over the month and 6.03% over the year, though it remains modestly below its June 2022 peak. The cross-section indicates that the renewed firmness is concentrated in commodities, mining, manufacturing, and transportation rather than in the stickier service categories. For forecasters and policymakers, that pattern argues for continued attention to pipeline inflation: even without a generalized price spike across all sectors, renewed goods-side pressure can complicate the outlook for disinflation and real activity.
