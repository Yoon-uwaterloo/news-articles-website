Inflation after the 2022 Surge: What Three Price Gauges Reveal in the Latest Data

Inflation data matter because they summarize how quickly households' purchasing power is being eroded and how much restraint monetary policy may still need to deliver. Using monthly observations from January 2021 through March 2026 for headline CPI, median CPI, and the PCE price index, this note asks a simple question in the spirit of real-time monitoring: how far has inflation cooled from its 2022 peak, and how broad-based is the remaining price pressure?

Background

The dataset contains three monthly indicators. Headline CPI (CPIAUCSL) is an index level for consumer prices and is the most widely cited inflation measure. Median CPI, published by the Cleveland Fed, is a monthly percent change measure intended to filter out extreme relative-price movements and provide a cleaner signal of underlying inflation. PCEPI is the Federal Reserve's preferred broad inflation gauge because it uses chain weighting and a wider scope of expenditures. All three series begin in January 2021 and extend through March 2026, although headline CPI has one missing observation in October 2025.

To compare these series on common footing, the analysis computes month-over-month and year-over-year inflation rates for CPI and PCE, uses median CPI as reported on a monthly percent-change basis, and annualizes it when benchmarking underlying inflation. The sample is short by macroeconomic standards, but it still spans the post-pandemic inflation run-up, the 2022 crest, and the subsequent disinflationary period.

Findings

As shown in the chart below, both headline CPI and PCE inflation rose sharply through 2021 and 2022, then decelerated materially. CPI year-over-year inflation peaked at 8.98 percent in June 2022, while PCE inflation reached its sample high of 7.24 percent in June 2022. By March 2026, CPI inflation had reaccelerated to 3.29 percent and PCE inflation to 3.50 percent, both well below their peaks but still above a 2 percent benchmark.

The next chart shades months when CPI inflation was at or above 5 percent, a practical proxy for the high-inflation regime within this limited sample. That regime runs mainly from mid-2021 through late 2023. The shaded period captures the broad inflation surge and illustrates that the latest reading, while elevated relative to target-consistent inflation, is substantially below the most acute phase of the episode.

Because the dataset does not contain an official composite index, I construct a simple composite signal as the average of CPI year-over-year inflation, PCE year-over-year inflation, and median CPI expressed at a 12-month rate. The bar chart below shows how each component deviates from that composite. During the 2022 surge, headline CPI tended to run above the composite, while PCE was usually lower and median CPI often sat between the two. More recently, the three measures have converged, suggesting less dispersion across inflation gauges even though inflation remains above pre-surge norms.

The recent two-year window highlights the slowdown more clearly. CPI inflation averaged 2.79 percent over the last twenty-four months, compared with 8.00 percent in 2022. PCE inflation averaged 2.67 percent over the same recent window, down from 6.54 percent in 2022. Still, the most recent three-month annualized rates point to some firming: CPI's three-month annualized pace was 5.33 percent in March 2026, while the comparable PCE rate was 5.61 percent.

Cross-indicator comparisons reinforce the message that the three gauges are tracking a common inflation process. CPI and PCE year-over-year inflation have a correlation of 0.992 in this sample. The close alignment is visible in the scatter plot below, although CPI generally prints somewhat higher than PCE, reflecting known methodological differences in scope and weights.

The distribution of monthly changes shows that the inflation surge was not only about a few outlier months, but also about a rightward shift in the overall distribution of monthly price increases. The latest monthly readings remain moderate rather than minimal: in March 2026, CPI increased 0.87 percent month over month, PCE increased 0.66 percent, and median CPI rose 0.19 percent, or about 2.28 percent at an annualized rate.

Historical Comparison

Even within a sample that begins after the pandemic recession, the recent disinflation is notable. Average CPI inflation stepped down from 8.00 percent in 2022 to 4.15 percent in 2023, 2.95 percent in 2024, and 2.69 percent in 2025, before moving to 2.70 percent year to date in 2026. The comparable sequence for PCE was 6.54, 3.81, 2.62, 2.63, and 3.06 percent. Relative to the post-2021 inflation crest, the current environment looks more like a late-stage normalization than a completed return to target-consistent inflation.

The data also show an important asymmetry: headline inflation cooled much faster than the underlying signal from median CPI during 2023, but median CPI itself has not collapsed. Its sample high was 0.631 percent in June 2022 on a monthly basis, and its latest reading of 0.190 percent remains above its sample minimum of 0.106 percent in January 2021. That pattern suggests broad-based inflation has eased, but not disappeared.

Turning Points and Anomalies

The most important turning point in the sample is the transition from acceleration in 2021-22 to disinflation in 2023. CPI year-over-year inflation fell from its June 2022 peak of 8.98 percent to 2.33 percent in April 2025, before edging up again more recently. The single missing CPI observation in October 2025 is a data limitation rather than an economic turning point and should be treated cautiously in any high-frequency interpretation.

Caveats and Limitations

Several cautions are in order. First, the sample begins in 2021, so it does not include pre-pandemic or earlier recession periods directly; historical comparisons therefore refer to phases within the post-pandemic inflation cycle rather than to longer-run benchmarks. Second, one CPI observation is missing in October 2025, which slightly affects rolling calculations and recent-change measures. Third, median CPI is reported here as a monthly percent change, whereas CPI and PCE are index levels, so comparisons require transformation and should be interpreted as approximations rather than exact apples-to-apples matches. Finally, no official recession indicator is included in the dataset, so the shaded regime chart marks high-inflation episodes rather than NBER recessions.

Conclusion

The broad message from these data is that the inflation shock of 2021-22 has diminished substantially but not fully run its course. Headline CPI and PCE inflation are far below their peaks, and the dispersion across inflation gauges has narrowed. Yet current readings remain above a 2 percent benchmark, and the latest three-month annualized rates suggest that the final leg of disinflation may be uneven. For forecasters and policymakers, that combination argues for continued monitoring of both headline and underlying inflation measures rather than relying on any single release.
